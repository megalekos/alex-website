Project:
Haaartland

Background and Challenge

Haaartland is a community platform with apps for web and mobile. Users had a hard time signing up as members and to build their digital community unaided.
The project was for three weeks and fully remote. As a UX researcher and designer, I was to improve flows and wayfinding, while a dedicated desginer took care of visuals and UI. I worked with the Design Thinking process.

Result

Optmised flows through shorter user journeys and a tutorial that teaches newcomers how to set up their community.

Process

Empathize

Starting the Design Thinking process I was to collect data on users and the problem at hand:

  • Why was it hard to register?
  • And how could we aid in building a community?

Due to the project’s short span however, I decided to iterate the research phases (empathize + define) whenever I had the oportunity, rather than having a strict research stage in the beginning.

I went through the registration process and steps for building a community as if I were a user, mapping out issues.

These I turned into a journey map in Miro. This way I could analyse without preconceptions and familiarise myself with the product. To deepen my knowledge, I did a stakeholder interview with a project leader.

Ideate

To find the best way of presenting the step-by-step tutorial for the community building tool, I did a remote workshop in Figma with a developer. He knew all technical constraints that we had to work with.

Prototype

I made the prototype a lo-fi wireframe and non-clickable to save time, and because it is sufficiant for trying out the idea.

I did two preliminary tests with this setup, which confirmed the format was good. Then I updated the prototype into a clickable one before the next phase.

Testing and Empathy 2

Two business customers were now available for remote user testing. The interactive prototype paired with interviews taught me:

  1. The users wanted to do things in another order.
  2. Previous knowledge on digital communities differs between users.

Conclusion

This meant that, for the final handover, I proposed splitting the community building into two sessions, making for a more rapid start and with the user’s prioritized tasks in the first session.
